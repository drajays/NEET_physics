Forces — Newton's Laws of Motion

The whole force toolkit for NEET: Newton's three laws, weight & normal reaction, tension, springs, friction, inclines, connected bodies, pulleys, pseudo-force and circular-motion forces — key ideas, worked examples, a full formula sheet, 30 NEET numericals and an unlimited practice quiz.

Key ideas at a glance

Newton's three laws

  • 1st: if ΣF = 0 then a = 0 (rest or uniform velocity).
  • 2nd: ΣF = ma = dp/dt.
  • 3rd: action = −reaction, on different bodies (never cancel).

Weight & normal reaction

  • Weight W = mg, always vertically down.
  • Flat surface: N = mg.
  • Push up with F: N = mg − F; press down: N = mg + F.
  • On incline: N = mg cosθ.

Lift (apparent weight)

  • Up with a: N = m(g + a) — feel heavier.
  • Down with a: N = m(g − a) — feel lighter.
  • Free fall (a = g): N = 0 → weightless.

Friction

  • Static adjusts: fs ≤ μsN.
  • Kinetic (sliding): fk = μkN, usually μs > μk.
  • Opposes relative motion (or its tendency).

Inclined plane

  • Smooth: a = g sinθ down.
  • Rough, sliding down: a = g(sinθ − μcosθ).
  • Stays put while tanθ ≤ μs; angle of repose tanθr = μs.

Pulleys & connected bodies

  • Atwood: a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂).
  • Tension: T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂).
  • Block + hanging mass: a = m₂g/(m₁+m₂).

Pseudo force (non-inertial)

  • Magnitude Fp = ma₀, opposite the frame's acceleration.
  • Hanging bob: tanθ = a/g, geff = √(g²+a²).

Watch out

  • Action & reaction act on different bodies — don't cancel them in one FBD.
  • Friction is whatever is needed up to μN — not always μN.
  • Tension is one value throughout a massless string over an ideal pulley.

Worked examples

1 — Force at an angle: 20 kg block, F = 100 N at 37° above horizontal, μk = 0.25
Vertical: N = mg − F sin37° = 200 − 100×0.6 = 140 N.
Friction: f = μN = 0.25×140 = 35 N.
Horizontal: F cos37° = 100×0.8 = 80 N ⇒ net = 80 − 35 = 45 N.
a = 45/20 = 2.25 m/s² (lifting the block reduces N, so friction drops).
2 — Minimum pull to start a block (angle of least effort)
Pulling at angle θ: F = μmg/(cosθ + μ sinθ).
This is smallest when tanθ = μ, giving Fmin = μmg/√(1+μ²).
So a slight upward pull beats a purely horizontal one — it both pulls and unloads the surface.
3 — Atwood machine: m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 5 kg (g = 10)
a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂) = (5−3)×10/8 = 2.5 m/s².
T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂) = 2×3×5×10/8 = 37.5 N.
Check: for m₂, m₂g − T = 50 − 37.5 = 12.5 = m₂a = 5×2.5. ✓
4 — Block on a rough table pulled by a hanging mass
m₁ on table (μ), m₂ hanging: a = g(m₂ − μm₁)/(m₁+m₂).
With m₁ = 5, m₂ = 2, μ = 0.2: a = 10(2 − 0.2×5)/7 = 10(1)/7 = 10/7 ≈ 1.43 m/s².
Motion happens only if m₂ > μsm₁.
5 — Vertical circle: minimum speeds for a full loop
At the top, gravity alone can supply the centripetal need when mg = mv²/r ⇒ vtop,min = √(gr).
Energy conservation from top to bottom (drop 2r): vbottom,min = √(5gr).
Tension at the lowest point: T = mg + mv²/r (largest there).
6 — Lift & apparent weight: 60 kg person, a = 2 m/s² up (g = 10)
N = m(g + a) = 60×12 = 720 N (vs 600 N at rest) — the floor pushes harder, so you feel heavier.
If the cable snapped (a = g down): N = m(g − g) = 0 → apparent weightlessness.
7 — Pseudo force: pendulum in an accelerating train
In the train's frame a pseudo force ma₀ acts backward on the bob.
It settles at tanθ = a₀/g from the vertical, along the effective gravity geff = √(g²+a₀²).
For a₀ = 5, g = 10: tanθ = 0.5 ⇒ θ ≈ 26.6°.
8 — Stopping distance from friction
Only friction decelerates: a = μg, so s = u²/(2μg) and t = u/(μg).
Note the mass cancels — a heavy and a light block on the same surface stop in the same distance.
9 — Two hydrogen atoms: how many electric forces? (HC Verma 4-W1)
The system has 4 charged particles — two protons (nuclei) and two electrons.
Each particle exerts an electric force on the other three, so the total number of forces is 4 × 3 = 12 forces.
(Counting ordered pairs; the 12 come in 6 action–reaction pairs — like charges repel, unlike attract.)
10 — Force between two charged rods, and the l ≫ a and a ≫ l limits (HC Verma 4-W3)
Two rods of length l lie end-to-end with a gap a; each carries +q on its left end and −q on its right end.
Adding the attractions/repulsions between the four end-charges, the net force on rod A (towards the right) is
F = (q²/4πε₀)·[ 1/a² − 2/(l+a)² + 1/(2l+a)² ].
If l ≫ a: the last two terms are negligible ⇒ F = q²/(4πε₀a²) (only the nearest +q, −q pair across the gap matters).
If a ≫ l: all three terms → 1/a² ⇒ F ≈ (q²/4πε₀)[1/a² − 2/a² + 1/a²] = 0 (each rod looks like a neutral dipole from far away).

Shortcuts & standard angle values

Angle of repose = the friction angle

  • A block stays on an incline while tanθ ≤ μs.
  • At the verge of sliding, tanθr = μs — read μ straight off the tilt!
  • μ = 1 ⇒ θr = 45°; μ = 1/√3 ⇒ θr = 30°; μ = √3 ⇒ θr = 60°.

3-4-5 trig (37° & 53°)

  • θsincostan
    30°1/2√3/21/√3
    37°3/54/53/4
    45°1/√21/√21
    53°4/53/54/3
    60°√3/21/2√3
  • 37° and 53° turn ugly inclines into clean 3-4-5 arithmetic.

Least-effort pull

  • Pull at tanθ = μ (not horizontal) to drag a block with least force.
  • Then Fmin = μmg/√(1+μ²).

Mass cancels — remember these

  • Smooth incline: a = g sinθ (no m).
  • Friction stop: s = u²/2μg (no m).
  • Sliding down rough incline: a = g(sinθ − μcosθ) (no m).

Pseudo force in an accelerating train — 11 NEET MCQs

Why geff = √(g² + a²)?  In the train's (non-inertial) frame two accelerations act on a bob: true gravity g straight down and the pseudo acceleration a₀ straight back — at 90°. The Law of Cosines R = √(A² + B² + 2AB cosθ) with cos90° = 0 kills the cross-term, leaving geff = √(g² + a₀²). The bob hangs along this diagonal at tanθ = a₀/g from the vertical. (Adding them as 10 + 5 = 15 would wrongly assume cos0° = 1; ignoring a₀ wrongly drops a real force.)

Standing up in a lurching train comes down to three defences: friction (feet don't slide), torque balance (you don't topple) and tension (you grab a handle). Each MCQ below isolates one.
