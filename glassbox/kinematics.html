Rest and Motion — Kinematics

Distance vs displacement, average & instantaneous speed/velocity, and reading v–t graphs — the core ideas from Chapter 3 (HC Verma), worked examples, and an unlimited NEET-UG practice quiz.

Key ideas at a glance

Distance vs displacement

  • Distance = length of the actual path (scalar, ≥ 0).
  • Displacement = straight line from start to end (vector).
  • Semicircle radius R: distance = πR, displacement = 2R.
  • Full circle: distance = 2πR, displacement = 0.

Average speed & velocity

  • Average speed = total distance / total time.
  • Average velocity = displacement / total time.
  • For two equal-distance halves at v₁, v₂: avg speed = 2v₁v₂/(v₁+v₂).

Instantaneous speed

  • v = ds/dt (slope of the s–t graph).
  • If s = k t², then v = 2k t.

v–t graph

  • Slope of v–t graph = acceleration.
  • Area under the v–t graph = distance travelled.
  • Triangle: distance = ½ × base × height.

Uniform acceleration

  • v = u + at
  • s = ut + ½at²
  • v² = u² + 2as

Watch out

  • Average velocity can be zero while average speed is not (round trips).
  • Displacement direction points from initial → final position.

Worked examples (HC Verma Ch. 3)

3.1 — Semicircular morning walk, R = 40.0 m
Distance = length of the track = πR = π × 40.0 = 126 m.
Displacement = straight line joining the ends = diameter = 2R = 2 × 40.0 = 80 m, directed from start to finish.
3.2 — s = (2.5 m/s²)t²: average speed (0→5 s) and instantaneous speed at 5 s
(a) Distance in 5 s: s = 2.5 × (5.0)² = 62.5 m.
Average speed = 62.5 m / 5.0 s = 12.5 m/s.
(b) v = ds/dt = (2.5)(2t) = (5.0 m/s²)t. At t = 5.0 s, v = 5.0 × 5.0 = 25 m/s.
3.3 — Distance from a v–t graph (triangle: height 6 m/s, base 3 s)
Distance = area under the v–t graph = area of the right triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 3 s × 6 m/s = 9 m.
3.4 — Minute hand 4.0 cm: average velocity of the tip
Over 30 minutes the tip moves from the 12 to the 6 mark, so displacement = diameter = 2R = 8.0 cm.
(a) 6:00→6:30 a.m. = 1800 s: vav = 8.0 / 1800 = 4.4 × 10⁻³ cm/s.
(b) 6:00 a.m.→6:30 p.m. = 45000 s: vav = 8.0 / 45000 = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ cm/s.
(Displacement is the same; only the time differs.)
3.5 — Uniform acceleration: u = 2.5 m/s, a = 0.50 m/s²
(a) Distance in the first 2 s — use x = ut + ½at²:
x = (2.5)(2) + ½(0.50)(2)² = 5.0 + 1.0 = 6.0 m. Since the particle never turns back, this is also the distance travelled.
(b) Time to reach v = 7.5 m/s — use v = u + at:
t = (v − u)/a = (7.5 − 2.5)/0.50 = 10 s.
(c) Distance to reach v = 7.5 m/s — use v² = u² + 2ax:
x = (v² − u²)/(2a) = (56.25 − 6.25)/(1.00) = 50 m.
3.6 — Displacement in the last 1 second (and the "tᵗʰ second" caveat)
Position at time t: s = ut + ½at². Position at (t − 1 s): s′ = u(t−1) + ½a(t−1)².
Displacement in the last second: st = s − s′ = u(1 s) + (a/2)(2t − 1 s)(1 s). …(i)
For u = 5 m/s, a = 2 m/s², t = 10 s:
st = (5)(1) + ½(2)(2×10 − 1)(1) = 5 + (1)(19)(1) = 24 m.
Correction / caveat: dropping the "(1 s)" factors gives the popular formula sₜ = u + (a/2)(2t − 1), which is dimensionally inconsistent (its terms have different dimensions). Equation (i) is the correct form. Also, this is the displacement in the last second, not necessarily the distance covered.
3.7 — Ball thrown up at 4.0 m/s: maximum height (g = 10 m/s²)
Take up as +Y, so u = +4.0 m/s and a = −g = −10 m/s². At the highest point the velocity is v = 0.
Use v² = u² + 2ay: 0 = (4.0)² + 2(−10)y.
y = 16 / 20 = 0.80 m.
(Key idea: at maximum height the velocity is momentarily zero; sign convention makes a = −g.)
3.8 — 2-D constant acceleration: a = 1.5 m/s² at 37°, u = 8.0 m/s along X
Resolve the acceleration (cos37° = 0.8, sin37° = 0.6):
aₓ = 1.5 cos37° = 1.2 m/s²; a_y = 1.5 sin37° = 0.90 m/s². Initial: uₓ = 8.0 m/s, u_y = 0.
Velocity at t = 4.0 s: vₓ = 8.0 + 1.2×4 = 12.8 m/s; v_y = 0 + 0.90×4 = 3.6 m/s.
Speed v = √(12.8² + 3.6²) = 13.3 m/s, at tanθ = v_y/vₓ = 3.6/12.8 = 9/32 (θ ≈ 15.7° above X).
Position at t = 4.0 s: x = 8.0×4 + ½×1.2×4² = 41.6 m; y = 0 + ½×0.90×4² = 7.2 m.
So the particle is at (41.6 m, 7.2 m).
3.9 — Range of a projectile: 12.0 m/s at 45° (g = 10 m/s²)
Horizontal range R = u²sin2θ/g = (12)²·sin(2×45°)/10.
Since sin90° = 1: R = 144 × 1 / 10 = 14.4 m.
(Pro-tip: 45° gives sin2θ = 1, so the range is maximum at this angle.)
3.10 — River swimmer: 4.0 km/h across a 3.0 km/h current
Swimmer aims perpendicular to the flow, so the two velocities are at 90°.
vS,G = √(vS,R² + vR,G²) = √(4.0² + 3.0²) = 5.0 km/h (a 3-4-5 triangle).
Angle with the flow direction: tanθ = 4.0/3.0 = 4/3 ⇒ θ = 53°.
3.11 — Rain–man: walk 3.0 km/h, rain falls 4.0 km/h vertically
vrain,man = vrain,street − vman,street (flip the man's velocity and add).
vrain,man = √(4.0² + 3.0²) = 5.0 km/h (again a 3-4-5 triangle).
Angle with the vertical: tanθ = 3.0/4.0 = 3/4 ⇒ θ = 37°. The man tilts his umbrella 37° forward.
Bonus — Relative acceleration between two frames
Differentiating the relative-velocity relation: aP,S = aP,S′ + aS′,S.
If frame S′ moves with uniform velocity relative to S, then aS′,S = 0, so aP,S = aP,S′ — the acceleration of a body is the same in both frames.
Relative acceleration appears only when a frame is itself accelerating (a non-inertial frame).

Worked-out problems (HC Verma Ch. 3, set 1–15)

1 — Average speed: 6 km/h for 1 km, then 8 km/h for 1 km
Equal distances ⇒ harmonic mean. Time = 1/6 + 1/8 = 7/24 h for 2 km.
Average speed = 2 / (7/24) = 48/7 ≈ 6.9 km/h.
2 — Teacher: 10 rounds of a 40 ft wall in 50 min
Total distance = 10 × 2 × 40 = 800 ft ⇒ average speed = 800/50 = 16 ft/min.
Starts and ends at the same door ⇒ displacement = 0 ⇒ average velocity = 0.
3 — x = At³ + Bt² + Ct + D (A=1, B=4, C=−2, D=5, SI)
(a) Each term has dimension of length: [A]=LT⁻³, [B]=LT⁻², [C]=LT⁻¹, [D]=L.
(b) v = 3At² + 2Bt + C; at t=4 s: 3(16)+2(4)(4)−2 = 78 m/s.
(c) a = 6At + 2B; at t=4 s: 6(4)+2(4) = 32 m/s².
(d) x(0)=5, x(4)=125 ⇒ displacement 120 m ⇒ avg velocity = 120/4 = 30 m/s.
(e) v(0)=C=−2, v(4)=78 ⇒ avg acceleration = (78−(−2))/4 = 20 m/s².
4 — Describe motion from a v–t graph; areas & slopes
0→1 s speeds up (+x), 1→2 s slows to rest, 2→4 s moves in −x and returns to rest.
(a) 0–2 s distance = ½·2·10 = 10 m; (b) 2–4 s = 10 m (−x); (c) total distance = 20 m; (d) displacement = 10 − 10 = 0.
(e) a at t=½ s = slope OA = 10 m/s²; (f) a at t=2 s = slope = −10 m/s².
5 — From rest, constant a; v=100 m/s at t, v=150 m/s at t+1
(a) a = (150 − 100)/1 = 50 m/s².
(b) Over that 1 s: v² = u² + 2ax ⇒ 150² = 100² + 2(50)x ⇒ x = 125 m.
6 — Catapult: stone reaches 2.2 m/s over 24 cm
u=0, v=2.2 m/s, x=0.24 m. a = v²/(2x) = 4.84/0.48 = 10.1 m/s².
7 — Police chase: prove the pickpocket is caught if v ≥ √(2ad)
Motorcycle: s = ½at². Jeep: s + d = vt. So ½at² − vt + d = 0 ⇒ t = [v ± √(v² − 2ad)]/a.
t is real (a catch occurs) only if v² − 2ad ≥ 0, i.e. v ≥ √(2ad).
8 — Fly between an approaching car (40 km/h) and a wall (fly 100 km/h)
(a) Car covers 20 km in 20/40 = 0.5 h; the fly flies the whole time: 100 × 0.5 = 50 km.
(b) Each round trip leaves the car at 3/7 of its previous distance, never reaching 0 in finite steps ⇒ the fly makes an infinite number of trips.
9 — Ball dropped from 19.6 m, rebounds to same height (x, v, a plots)
Down: x = ½gt² = 4.9t². Reaches ground (x=19.6 m) at t = 2 s; back to start at t = 4 s.
x: 0, 4.9, 19.6, 4.9, 0 at t = 0,1,2,3,4 s. v: 0→+19.6 m/s (0–2 s) then jumps to −19.6→0 (2–4 s).
a = +9.8 m/s² throughout (except the instant of the bounce at t = 2 s).
10 — Stone dropped from a balloon rising at 5.0 m/s, 50 m high (g=10)
Up +ve: x=−50, u=5, a=−10 ⇒ −50 = 5t − 5t² ⇒ t = (1+√41)/2 = 3.7 s.
Balloon rises 5 × 3.7 = 18.5 m ⇒ height = 50 + 18.5 = 68.5 m.
11 — Football: 20 m/s at 45° (g=10)
uₓ = u_y = 10√2 m/s. (a) T = 2u_y/g = 2(10√2)/10 = 2.8 s.
(b) H = u_y²/2g = 200/20 = 10 m. (c) Range = uₓ·T = 10√2 · 2√2 = 40 m.
12 — Helicopter (speed u, altitude H) drops a packet
Vertical: H = ½gt² ⇒ t = √(2H/g). Horizontal drop point: D = u√(2H/g).
Straight-line distance to the victim at release = √(2u²H/g + H²).
13 — Horizontal projection (speed u) from top of an incline θ
x = ut, y = ½gt², and on the plane y = x tanθ ⇒ x = 2u²tanθ/g.
Distance along the plane: AP = (2u²/g)·tanθ·secθ.
14 — Projectile speed when its direction makes angle α
Horizontal velocity is constant: v cosα = u cosθ ⇒ v = u cosθ · secα.
15 — Bullet fired horizontally at an object that starts falling — does it hit?
Both the bullet and the object fall the same vertical distance ½gt² in the same time t.
So when the bullet reaches the object's vertical line, both have dropped equally ⇒ the bullet always hits the object (independent of u and g).
16 — Swim 3 km/h across a 2 km/h river to the point directly opposite (width 500 m)
To land directly opposite, the upstream component must cancel the flow.
(a) x-axis (along flow): 0 = −3 sinθ + 2 ⇒ sinθ = 2/3 ⇒ θ ≈ 41.8° from the perpendicular (aim upstream).
(b) Across-speed vm,g = 3 cosθ = √5 km/h. Time = 0.5/√5 = √5/10 h ≈ 0.224 h (≈ 13.4 min).
17 — Swim at a fixed 120° to a 2 km/h flow (3 km/h, width 500 m)
At 120° to the flow the across-component is 3 cos30° = 3√3/2 km/h.
(a) Time = 0.5 / (3√3/2) = 1/(3√3) h ≈ 0.192 h (≈ 11.5 min).
(b) Along-flow velocity = −3 sin30° + 2 = 0.5 km/h ⇒ drift = 0.5 × 1/(3√3) = 1/(6√3) km ≈ 96 m downstream.
18 — Umbrella at 30°; running 10 km/h makes rain vertical
Rain (w.r.t. ground) is 30° from vertical. Horizontal: vr,g sin30° = 10 ⇒ vr,g = 20 km/h.
Vertical (= rain w.r.t. man): vr,m = vr,g cos30° = 20·(√3/2) = 10√3 ≈ 17.3 km/h.
19 — Rain vertical at 8 km/h; at 12 km/h it tilts 30° — find the rain's true velocity
At 8 km/h rain looks vertical ⇒ vr,road sinα = 8. At 12 km/h: vr,road sin(30°+α) = 12 cos30°.
Dividing: sin(30°+α)/sinα = 3√3/4 ⇒ cotα = √3/2 (α ≈ 49°).
vr,road = 8/sinα = 4√7 ≈ 10.6 km/h, at α ≈ 49° to the vertical.
20 — Three particles chasing on an equilateral triangle (side d, speed v)
By symmetry they spiral into the centroid. The separation AB shrinks at rate v + v cos60° = 3v/2 (B's component toward A).
Time to meet = d / (3v/2) = 2d/3v.

Shortcuts & memory tricks

The √n / 2 rule for sin θ

  • Count n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 under a root and divide by 2:
  • θ√n/2sin θ
    0°√0/20
    30°√1/21/2
    45°√2/21/√2
    60°√3/2√3/2
    90°√4/21
  • For cos θ, just read the table bottom-to-top (cos0°=1, cos30°=√3/2, …).

Projectile max height scales as sin²θ

  • H = u²sin²θ/2g, and the "full height" (straight up, 90°) is Hmax = u²/2g.
  • So the height reached is just (sin²θ) × Hmax:
  • θsin²θHeight
    0°00
    30°1/4¼ Hmax
    45°1/2½ Hmax
    60°3/4¾ Hmax
    90°1Hmax
  • Example: straight up → 100 m. At 60° → ¾ × 100 = 75 m in 5 seconds, no pen needed!

The four means — NEET shortcut toolkit

Which mean?  Equal chunks of time → Arithmetic mean  ·  equal chunks of distance → Harmonic mean  ·  effect depends on the square of the quantity → RMS  ·  exponential decay / resonance → Geometric mean.
MeanFormulaWhen to useNEET chapter
Arithmetic(v₁+v₂)/2Equal time intervalsKinematics
Harmonic2v₁v₂/(v₁+v₂)Equal distance intervalsKinematics
RMS√[(v₁²+v₂²)/2]Effect ∝ (quantity)²KTG, AC circuits
Geometric√(a₁a₂)Proportional decay / resonanceOscillations, LCR

★ Key fact: for unequal numbers, AM > GM > HM, and RMS ≥ AM — so RMS is the largest of the four.
