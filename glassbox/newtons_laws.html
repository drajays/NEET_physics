Newton's Laws of Motion

HC Verma Chapter 5 for NEET: the three laws, momentum & impulse, free-body diagrams, lifts, Atwood machines, inclines, blocks in contact, pseudo force and momentum conservation (recoil, rockets) — key ideas, worked examples, a formula sheet, 30 high-yield Q&A and an unlimited quiz.

Key ideas at a glance

The three laws

  • 1st (inertia): ΣF = 0 ⇒ a = 0; defines an inertial frame.
  • 2nd: F = dp/dt = ma.
  • 3rd: FAB = −FBA — equal, opposite, on different bodies.

Momentum & impulse

  • p = mv ; F = dp/dt.
  • Impulse J = ∫F dt = Δp = area under the F–t graph.
  • No external force ⇒ total momentum conserved.

Common forces

  • Weight W = mg (down). Normal N ⟂ surface.
  • Tension T pulls along the string, away from the body.
  • Spring F = −kx.

Lift (apparent weight)

  • Up: N = m(g + a). Down: N = m(g − a).
  • Constant v: N = mg. Free fall: N = 0.

Pulleys & contact

  • Atwood: a = (m₁−m₂)g/(m₁+m₂); T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂).
  • Blocks in contact (F on m₁): a = F/(m₁+m₂), contact = m₂F/(m₁+m₂).

Pseudo force (non-inertial)

  • In a frame accelerating at a₀: add Fpseudo = −ma₀.
  • Hanging bob: tanθ = a₀/g. Block fixed on a wedge: a = g tanθ.

FBD recipe

  • Isolate one body; draw only the forces on it.
  • Pick axes (along motion / incline); write ΣF = ma per axis.
  • Constraint: bodies on one string share |a|.

Watch out

  • Action & reaction never cancel — different bodies.
  • Internal forces can't move the centre of mass.
  • On a flat curve / many results, mass cancels.

Worked examples (the tricky ones)

Painter in a hanging chair pulls himself up (HC Verma classic)
A 60 kg painter sits in a 20 kg chair and pulls the rope over a fixed pulley to rise at 1 m/s² (g = 10).
The single rope pulls up on the chair (T) and up on his hands (T), so the upward force on the (man + chair) system is 2T.
2T − (m+M)g = (m+M)a ⇒ 2T = 80(10+1) = 880 ⇒ T = 440 N.
Block held still on an accelerating smooth wedge
A block rests on a smooth wedge of incline θ. What horizontal acceleration a keeps it stationary relative to the wedge?
In the wedge frame a pseudo force ma acts back. Along the incline: ma cosθ = mg sinθ ⇒ a = g tanθ.
(Equivalently, the effective gravity must be ⟂ to the incline surface.)
Block pulled by a heavy (massive) rope
A block M is pulled along a smooth floor by a rope of mass m with force F at the far end.
Whole system: a = F/(M+m). The force the rope exerts on the block is only what accelerates M:
Fon block = Ma = MF/(M+m) < F — the rope keeps some force to accelerate itself.
Rocket lift-off: minimum gas ejection rate
Thrust from ejecting gas at speed vex: F = vex·(dm/dt).
To just lift a 1000 kg rocket (g = 10): F = mg ⇒ 500·(dm/dt) = 10000 ⇒ dm/dt = 20 kg/s.
Tug-of-war: rope suddenly cut
While the rope holds, team A is in equilibrium: the rope pulls them with T, balanced by ground friction T (the other way).
The instant the rope is cut, the rope's pull T vanishes but the friction can't change instantly, leaving a net force T on team A (they lurch backward).
Three forces in equilibrium — remove one
3 N, 4 N and 5 N keep a 2 kg particle in equilibrium, so the 3 N and 4 N resultant is 5 N opposing the 5 N force.
Remove the 5 N force and the remaining 3 N & 4 N give a 5 N net force ⇒ a = F/m = 5/2 = 2.5 m/s².
